PREFACE The nationwide WPA Historical Records Survey was organized in l935 by Dr. Luther H. Evans to inventory state and local archives, early American imprints, church archives and collections of manuscripts. Since March l, l940, Sargent B. Child has been the National Director of the Program. In Louisiana, the Historical Records Survey now operates as a unit of the Statewide Records Project, which is sponsored by the Depart- ment of Archives, Louisiana State University, and some sixty local governmental agencies. This directory of churches in New Orleans, preliminary in form, is the initial publication in the Church Archives Inventory series, pre- pared by the Louisiana Historical Records Survey Project. A directory of existing churches in the State was begun to expedite the preparation of the church archives inventories by serving as a guide to future church archival work. It is planned that this directory will be revised and included shortly in a list of churches for the whole State. In its present form the directory locates and identifies some 800 congregations, organizations and institutions in the city of New 0rleans. All churches and or^anizations have been impartially listed as they were found in the field on the authority of responsible clerical leaders. The United States Census of Religious Bodies system of classification has been followed in most instances. Many means were employed and sources used in gathering the informa- tion for this preliminary directory, including official denominational directories, official minutes of conferences and conventions, annuals, journals, pamphlets, city directories, telephone directories, lists of non-taxable property, conveyance records, mortgage records, charters, newspapers, land-use maps, and personal interviews. All information has been checked as thoroughly as possible, We hope that errors and omissions will be promptly called to our attention by the clergy and laity in order that the statewide directory may carry an improved section on New 0rleans. The democratic system of church government enjoyed by the Baptist Church has presented a problem peculiar only to that denomination. While the white Baptists, as a rule, have only one local association and one national convention, the Negro Baptists have organized numerous churches and formed many affiliations. Some churches in the same association claim a different national affiliation; others have no national affilia- tions whatsosver, Kence, we have been forced, in the interests of clarity and brevity, to list the Negro Baptist churches under four headings, viz., THE NATIONAL BAPTIST CONVENTI0N 0F THE UNITED STATES 0F AMERICA, INC0RP0- RATED; THE NATI0NAL BAPTIST C0NVENTI0N 0F THE UNITED STATES; BAPTIST (those belonging to local associations but no national conventions); and BAPTIST--INDEPENDENT. This arrangement repeats many associations three times, but the presentation, we trust, is reasonably clear. The only alternative would be to indicate under each church its associational and conventional affiliations. This procedure would entail much repetition and the use of more space. / r iii
